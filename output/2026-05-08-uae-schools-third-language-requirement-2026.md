## Overview of the Third Language Requirement

The Central Board of Secondary Education (CBSE) has mandated that its affiliated [schools](https://www.schoolsery.com/schools), including those in the UAE, implement a new third language requirement (R3) for students in Grade 6 by May 31, 2026. This initiative is part of the board's preparations for the 2026-27 academic session under the National Curriculum Framework for School Education 2023.

## Implementation Timeline and Flexibility

CBSE has noted that while many schools have already selected and uploaded their language options via the OASIS portal, some institutions are still finalising their submissions. To facilitate a smooth transition, all schools must complete updates to their R3 language choices within the specified deadline. Notably, CBSE has allowed flexibility for schools located outside India, permitting them to offer one compulsory Indian language instead of the two typically required for schools within the country.

## Current Language Options and Challenges

As educators prepare for the implementation of this new requirement, they face several practical challenges. Many schools are actively consulting with various stakeholders, including school leadership and curriculum heads, to ensure that the rollout is structured and student-centred. For instance, institutions are working on securing qualified language teachers, adjusting timetables, and managing student workloads to align with the new guidelines.

Currently, students in many schools study English as their first language, with options for Hindi, French, and Malayalam as second languages. The introduction of the third language requirement adds another layer of complexity, as schools must navigate the operational implications of the new curriculum.

## Broader Educational Context

The introduction of the third language requirement is part of a broader evolution in the CBSE's approach to education, which includes the gradual rollout of a Global Curriculum aimed at catering to a diverse student population. This curriculum shift reflects a growing emphasis on native language learning while adapting to new educational directives.

Some school leaders have raised questions regarding how Arabic will fit into the new framework, particularly in terms of its classification as a native language in the context of UAE schools. Nevertheless, the overarching message from educators is one of reassurance, as they emphasise the importance of adapting to these changes over time.

## Implications for Parents

For parents, this transition presents both opportunities and challenges. The requirement for a third language adds to the academic framework, which may enhance language skills and cultural understanding among students. However, parents may also be concerned about the implications for curriculum balance, student workload, and the availability of qualified teachers.

As schools work towards compliance with CBSE’s new guidelines, parents are encouraged to stay informed about their child’s language options and the overarching educational goals. The successful implementation of this requirement will depend on effective communication between schools and families, ensuring that students are well-prepared for the evolving educational landscape.

In summary, as UAE schools gear up for the mandatory third language requirement by 2026, the focus will be on careful planning and execution to meet educational standards while supporting students' language development in a multicultural setting.
