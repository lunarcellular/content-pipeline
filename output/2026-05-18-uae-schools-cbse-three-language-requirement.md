## Overview of the Revised CBSE Language Policy

The Central Board of Secondary Education (CBSE) has introduced a revised framework that will require students in UAE [schools](https://www.schoolsery.com/schools) to study three languages starting from the 2026–27 academic year. This policy aims to align with India's National Education Policy (NEP) 2020 and the National Curriculum Framework for School Education (NCF-SE) 2023. The adaptation of this policy is particularly relevant for Indian students studying in various schools across the UAE.

## Flexibility in Language Requirements

One of the primary concerns surrounding the new language policy was the requirement for students to study two native Indian languages. However, recent clarifications from CBSE indicate that schools in the Gulf region will have flexibility in this regard. Under the new structure, students entering Grade 9 will have the option to study Arabic as their third language, designated as R3. Importantly, students will not be required to sit for a board examination in Arabic during Grade 10, which alleviates some academic pressure.

### Details of the Language Structure

The new language framework categorises the languages into R1, R2, and R3. R1 is expected to be English, while R2 can include languages such as Hindi, Malayalam, Urdu, or Tamil. The inclusion of Arabic as R3 provides a pathway for students in the UAE to maintain their connection to the local culture while fulfilling the requirements set by the CBSE.

## Implications for Non-Indian Students

Another important aspect of the revised policy is its impact on students of non-Indian nationalities who are enrolled in CBSE-affiliated schools. The previous requirements posed challenges for these students, leading to concerns among educators regarding their academic experience. The updated guidelines now ensure that only one Indian native language is mandatory for foreign institutions, providing relief for schools catering to a diverse student body.

## Exemptions and Considerations

The CBSE has also announced special considerations for Students of Determination and those transitioning between foreign schools and India. While the specific exemptions for these students are yet to be fully detailed, the adjustments reflect a growing recognition of the varied needs of students in international contexts. Schools are preparing to implement the three-language formula, but the effectiveness of this transition will depend on continued support from the CBSE.

## Future of Foreign Languages in UAE Schools

While the revised policy has addressed many concerns, some educators express apprehensions about the future of foreign languages, such as French, within the new framework. As schools adapt to the requirement of offering two native Indian languages, those that currently provide French may encounter difficulties in aligning their curricula with the new mandates. There is hope that the anticipated Global Curriculum by CBSE will incorporate provisions for such languages, catering to the diverse linguistic landscape of UAE schools.

## Conclusion: What This Means for Parents

The changes to the CBSE language policy signify a thoughtful adjustment designed to reduce academic pressure on students while accommodating the unique needs of schools in the UAE. For parents, this means a more adaptable educational environment for their children, particularly for those from non-Indian backgrounds. As schools prepare for these changes, parents can expect a continued focus on maintaining educational standards while fostering a multilingual learning experience. The implementation of this policy will be closely monitored, and its success will largely depend on how effectively schools can navigate these new requirements.
